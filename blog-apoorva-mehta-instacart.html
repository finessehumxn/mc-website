The Guy Who Failed 20 Times Before Your Groceries Showed Up

Apoorva Mehta flopped at 20 startups, then turned a grocery run into a billion-dollar empire.

Apoorva MehtaInstacart

Picture a guy who quit a cushy Amazon engineering gig, moved to San Francisco, and proceeded to face-plant on roughly twenty startup ideas. A social network for lawyers. An ad network. Stuff nobody remembers. That guy is Apoorva Mehta, and the twenty-first swing connected so hard it changed how a generation buys avocados.

From Twenty Flops to One Big Bet

Mehta grew up between India, Libya, and Canada, studied engineering, and landed at Amazon working on supply-chain logistics for fulfillment. Useful resume line, but he was restless. So in 2010 he bailed to chase the founder dream and spent two long years building things that, by his own cheerful admission, nobody wanted.

The lightbulb was almost embarrassingly simple: he hated grocery shopping. In 2012 he wrote the first version of the Instacart app himself and reportedly placed the very first order on his own platform, a bottle of hummus and a six-pack delivered to his door. The pitch wasn't sexy. It was groceries. But the timing, the smartphone, and the logistics brain were all quietly aligned.

The Genius Was the Boring Part

Here's the creative move most people miss: Instacart didn't buy warehouses or trucks. Mehta built an asset-light marketplace that layered software on top of stores that already existed. Shoppers were freelancers. The inventory was someone else's. The magic was the app stitching it together so cleanly that a customer felt like the supermarket had teleported into their pocket.

To get into the legendary Y Combinator accelerator after the application deadline, Mehta allegedly mailed a partner a six-pack of beer ordered through his own app. Cheeky, on-brand, and a perfect demo of the product in one move. They let him in.

The best founder stories aren't about the idea. They're about the twenty ideas that came first.

Pandemic Rocket Fuel and a Mega-IPO

For years Instacart was a solid-but-scrappy player. Then 2020 happened, the whole world panic-ordered toilet paper and oat milk from home, and the company became essential infrastructure overnight. Order volume reportedly exploded, partnerships multiplied, and Instacart cemented itself as a household name across North America.

By the time the company went public in 2023, Instacart was valued in the billions, and Mehta, who had stepped back from the CEO seat, walked away as the founder of one of the defining gig-economy platforms of the decade. Not bad for a guy whose original sales pitch was "I'll grab your milk."

What makes the story so deeply Millennial Creatives is that Mehta didn't out-invent anyone. He out-noticed them. He saw a chore everybody quietly resented and designed an experience smooth enough to make people pay extra to skip it. The creativity wasn't in the technology, it was in the empathy, the patience, and the willingness to look ridiculous twenty times before looking like a genius once.

The real flex of any builder isn't avoiding failure. It's surviving it long enough to ship the version that finally works.
