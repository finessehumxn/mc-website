The Dancer Who Turned Sweat Into a Unicorn

Payal Kadakia couldn't find a dance class online, so she built the app that books millions of them.

Payal KadakiaClassPass

The billion-dollar idea was born out of pure frustration. Payal Kadakia just wanted to take a ballet class. She sat at her computer, hunting through clunky studio websites, and realized booking a single workout was somehow harder than booking a flight across the planet. Most people would close the laptop and order takeout. Kadakia built a unicorn instead.

A Dancer With a Day Job

Kadakia grew up dancing, eventually founding her own Indian-American dance company while working a corporate strategy job. Movement was her oxygen, but the business world was her training ground. That collision, the artist who also reads a P&L, defined everything that came next.

Her first crack at the idea was a class-discovery search engine called Classtivity. It was logical, useful, and a commercial flop. People didn't want to search for classes; they wanted a frictionless reason to actually show up. So Kadakia did the hard, humbling thing: she scrapped the model and reinvented it. The pivot became ClassPass, a single membership that let you bounce between yoga, spin, boxing, and Pilates studios all over the city.

Designing for the Flake in All of Us

The genius was psychological as much as technological. ClassPass understood that the enemy of fitness isn't laziness, it's friction and monotony. One sleek app, one membership, infinite variety. The product was built around the very real human tendency to get bored and bail, turning commitment-phobia into a feature rather than a bug.

She didn't sell workouts. She sold the thrilling possibility that tomorrow you could try something completely new.

The app experience was clean and almost addictive, gamifying the act of trying new studios. For boutique fitness studios with empty off-peak slots, ClassPass filled seats they'd otherwise lose forever. It was a rare three-way win: users got variety, studios got revenue, and Kadakia sat in the middle of a booming marketplace.

From Empty Studios to Billion-Dollar Status

The thing scaled across cities, then continents. ClassPass raised major venture funding and eventually reached a reported valuation in the billions, earning coveted unicorn status and making Kadakia one of the most prominent women founders in tech. The model expanded into wellness, beauty, and recovery, and the company was later acquired by a larger fitness-tech player, an exit that validated the entire bet.

Kadakia weathered the brutal stretch when the pandemic emptied gyms overnight, a near-existential threat to a business literally built on showing up in person. The company adapted with digital and on-demand options and survived, a testament to how durable the underlying idea really was.

She's since become an author and advocate, preaching the gospel of turning your passion into your work without losing yourself in the grind, and channeling that energy back into the dance company she never abandoned. The artist and the operator, it turns out, were always the same person.

But the core lesson is etched in every booked spin class: she felt a tiny, everyday annoyance that millions of people quietly shared, and instead of shrugging it off, she refused to let it go. Then, crucially, she had the humility to throw out her first answer when it didn't work and build a better one. The best products start with a frustration you simply can't let go.
