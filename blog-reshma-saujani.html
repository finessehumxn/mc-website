Brave, Not Perfect: Reshma Saujani's Mission to Rewrite the Code

She lost an election and turned the loss into a movement that's taught hundreds of thousands of girls to code. Meet the activist-founder playing the long game.

Reshma SaujaniGirls Who Code & Moms First
Photo: Fuzheado · CC BY-SA 4.0 · Wikimedia

Most people would call running for Congress and losing badly a low point. Reshma Saujani calls it the best thing that ever happened to her. Because on the campaign trail, walking through schools across New York, she noticed something that would reroute her entire life: classroom after classroom of computer science students, and almost no girls in any of them. That observation became a movement.

The Come-Up: Losing Loud

Saujani, the daughter of refugees, built an impressive resume, Ivy League law degree, a Wall Street career, the whole prestigious package. Then she walked away from it to run for office, becoming one of the first Indian American women to run for Congress. She lost. Decisively. But instead of retreating, she leaned into the thing she'd witnessed during the campaign: the gender gap in tech started early, and nobody was fixing it.

So in 2012 she founded Girls Who Code, with a deceptively simple mission, close the gender gap in technology by teaching girls to code, one classroom and one summer program at a time.

The Creativity: Reframing Failure as a Feature

Here's where Saujani's genius shows. She understood that the barrier wasn't ability, it was conditioning. Girls are raised to be perfect; boys are raised to be brave. So she built a brand and a curriculum around a radical idea: teaching girls to be brave, not perfect, to take risks, mess up, and keep going, the exact mindset coding requires.

We're raising our girls to be perfect, and we're raising our boys to be brave. Reshma decided to flip the whole script.

That phrase became a rallying cry, a bestselling book, and a viral TED talk that reframed an entire conversation. The marketing genius was making the mission feel like a movement rather than a charity. Girls Who Code wasn't pitched as a sad statistic to fix. It was pitched as a sisterhood to join, complete with a vivid identity, ambassadors, and stories that spread far beyond tech circles.

Then she did it again. With Moms First (formerly the Marshall Plan for Moms), Saujani took on the impossible-seeming fight for paid leave, affordable childcare, and economic justice for mothers, applying the same playbook: turn a quiet crisis into a loud, branded, unignorable campaign.

The Growth: Scale Measured in Lives

Girls Who Code has reportedly reached hundreds of thousands of girls through its programs and clubs, building a pipeline of young women into computer science that simply didn't exist before. The "wealth" Saujani is building isn't measured the way a SaaS founder's is, it's measured in graduates, in shifted statistics, in a generation of girls who now see themselves in an industry that ignored them.

And make no mistake, this is founding. She spotted a market failure, built an organization to solve it, scaled it nationally, and created a movement with genuine cultural and economic weight. The product just happens to be opportunity.

From a crushing electoral loss to a nationwide force, Saujani proved that the best founders aren't always chasing valuations. Sometimes they're chasing a future that doesn't exist yet, and building it anyway.

The kicker: She lost an election and won something bigger, a generation of brave girls who refuse to wait their turn.
