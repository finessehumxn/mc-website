From Mall Counter to Makeup Mogul

He preached that makeup has no gender or size — then built a beauty brand that put its money where its mantra is.

Patrick StarrrOne/Size Beauty

Long before the brand, there was a guy at a makeup counter with a camera and a catchphrase: makeup is one size fits all. Patrick Starrr said it so often it stopped being a slogan and started being a business plan. Today that line is literally stamped on a beauty empire called One/Size — proof that if you repeat your values loudly enough, eventually you build the company that embodies them.

The Counter-to-Camera Come-Up

Starrr's origin is gloriously un-glamorous: he worked retail beauty, learning the craft hand-to-hand, face-to-face, one customer at a time. Then he pointed a camera at himself and started posting tutorials — warm, funny, technically sharp, and radically welcoming in an industry that often felt like a velvet rope.

The audience came fast, because he was selling more than technique. He was selling permission — the idea that makeup belongs to everyone, regardless of gender, size, or how closely you resemble a magazine cover. That inclusive energy turned him into one of beauty's most beloved creators and a fixture of the YouTube and Instagram makeup boom.

A Brand That Walks the Inclusive Talk

Plenty of influencers slap their name on a product and call it a brand. Starrr did something tighter: he built One/Size around a single, sticky idea baked right into the name. The hero products lean into longevity and performance — setting sprays and powders engineered to survive sweat, tears, dancing, and a full day of being looked at — because the audience demanded makeup that actually holds.

The branding is unapologetically bold and pink-forward, theatrical without being exclusionary, glam but in on the joke. Shade ranges aim wide, the messaging stays welcoming, and the whole world feels like an extension of Starrr's on-camera personality: high-energy, generous, a little extra in the best way. Even the product names wink at you, and the campaigns lean into the same warmth that made the tutorials feel like a friend doing your face before a night out — a tone you can't fake and competitors can't copy.

One size, every face. It's not just the name — it's the entire thesis the products are built to prove.

Scaling the Sparkle

One/Size landed major retail distribution and became a standout in the crowded creator-brand gold rush — a space littered with launches that fizzle. What helped it stick was that the brand felt necessary, not opportunistic: it filled a real want for high-performance, genuinely inclusive products with a point of view.

Starrr has spoken about the brand's strong commercial momentum, and within the broader beauty-creator economy — where successful labels reportedly fetch valuations and sales figures in the eye-watering range — One/Size has positioned itself as a serious player rather than a vanity project. The mogul arc is well underway.

The deeper win is cultural. Starrr helped normalize men in beauty and pushed inclusivity from a marketing buzzword toward an actual product standard. He didn't just join the industry; he expanded who it was allowed to be for.

The final beat: build a brand around a belief you actually live, and customers don't just buy it — they believe it right along with you.
