She Got Rejected by 100 Investors, Then Built Canva

Melanie Perkins taught design from her mom's living room. Now half the internet uses her app.

Melanie PerkinsCanva
Photo: Melanie Perkins · CC BY-SA 2.0 · Wikimedia

Every presentation you've ever sat through that didn't look like it was made in 1998? There's a decent chance it was built in Canva. The woman behind that quiet revolution is Melanie Perkins, who reportedly pitched her idea to around a hundred investors and got turned down by most of them before building one of the most valuable tech companies on the planet. Rejection, it turns out, is just data.

The Yearbook Origin Story

Perkins was a university student in Perth, Australia, teaching design software on the side to pay the bills. Watching students struggle through clunky, expensive, intimidating programs, she had a thought that sounds obvious now and sounded ridiculous then: what if design was easy?

Her first crack at it was Fusion Books, an online tool that let schools design their own yearbooks with drag-and-drop simplicity. Run from her mom's living room with her co-founder and now-husband Cliff Obrecht, it became one of the largest yearbook publishers in Australia. That scrappy business was the proof of concept for something far bigger.

The Hundred Nos

Armed with the yearbook success, Perkins set her sights on a tool that could do for everything what Fusion Books did for yearbooks. Getting Silicon Valley to listen was brutal. She flew to the US, crashed in spare rooms, and pitched investor after investor who didn't get it. The legend goes that it took roughly a hundred rejections before the funding finally came.

The idea wasn't to build software for designers. It was to make everyone feel like one. That's a much bigger market, and almost nobody else was chasing it.

When Canva launched in 2013, the magic was in the simplicity. Drag-and-drop everything. Gorgeous templates so you never started from a blank page. A free tier that hooked you, with premium features when you needed more. The product was so intuitive that people who'd never opened design software in their lives were suddenly making logos, posters, and decks that looked genuinely good.

From Living Room to Stratosphere

The growth has been staggering. Canva scaled to hundreds of millions of users around the world, from students to small businesses to the marketing teams at massive corporations. It reportedly reached a valuation in the tens of billions of dollars, making Perkins one of the wealthiest self-made women in tech and one of the few female founders to build a company at that scale.

The creativity isn't just in the app's clean interface, though that interface is a masterclass. It's in the whole philosophy. Canva democratized design, taking a skill that used to require expensive tools and years of training and handing it to anyone with a browser. The brand itself is friendly, colorful, and approachable, the exact opposite of the gatekept, intimidating design world it disrupted.

Perkins and Obrecht have also pledged to give away the vast majority of their wealth, channeling it toward eliminating poverty. Building a fortune is one thing. Deciding to give most of it away is a flex of a different kind entirely.

So the next time a hundred people tell you no, remember the student from Perth who kept the pitch deck open anyway, and built an empire on the radical idea that good design should belong to everyone.

The lesson: the biggest opportunities hide inside the things everyone assumes are too hard, until someone makes them easy.
