She Pitched a Hit Movie Before She Could Legally Drive

Marsai Martin turned a 10-year-old's idea into a studio deal and became Hollywood's youngest executive producer.

Marsai MartinGenius Productions
Photo: The White House · Public domain · Wikimedia

Most kids at ten are negotiating bedtime. Marsai Martin was negotiating with a film studio. The Black-ish star had an idea, a body-swap comedy with a twist, and instead of letting an adult run off with it, she demanded a producer credit. The result, the 2019 movie Little, made her the youngest executive producer in Hollywood history. She was fourteen by the time it hit theaters.

The Pitch Heard Round the Writers' Room

Martin grew up on the set of a hit ABC sitcom, which is basically a film-school masterclass with snacks. Watching the machinery of television up close, she clocked something: the people with real power weren't always in front of the camera. They were the ones who owned the idea.

So when she dreamed up a spin on Big, where a mean grown-up boss wakes up as her younger self, she didn't just suggest it. She pitched it, reportedly inspired by the Tom Hanks classic she loved as a kid. Universal said yes. A child not only sold a studio comedy but locked in an executive producer title, the kind of credit veterans spend decades chasing.

Building a Brand, Not Just a Resume

The genius part isn't one movie. It's what she did with the leverage. Martin launched Genius Productions and signed a first-look deal with Universal, turning a single win into a pipeline. She wasn't auditioning for opportunities anymore, she was manufacturing them, for herself and for the next generation of young Black creatives who rarely see themselves behind the desk.

Owning the idea beats starring in it. Marsai figured that out before most of us figured out a 401(k).

Her whole brand world is built on a quietly radical premise: young people, especially young women of color, deserve to be decision-makers, not just talent to be cast. Every interview, every project, every credit reinforces it. That consistency is its own kind of marketing, the kind money can't fake.

The Long Game of a Mogul-in-Training

Since Little, Martin has stacked producing and voice roles, expanded her deals, and kept positioning herself as a creator first and a star second. The reported earnings and net worth that get tossed around in headlines are impressive for anyone, let alone someone who can't yet rent a car without a surcharge. But the more interesting number is the count of projects she now greenlights.

What Millennial Creatives loves here is the sheer audacity of the strategy. Martin understood ownership at an age when most of us were still trading lunch snacks. She treated her own taste as an asset worth protecting and built a company around it. That's not luck. That's vision with a contract attached.

The future of media won't belong to whoever's most famous. It'll belong to whoever owns the idea, and Marsai called that shot before puberty.
