The Razor Revolution Silicon Valley Slept On

Tristan Walker built a grooming empire for the people the beauty aisle forgot, then sold it to a giant.

Tristan WalkerWalker & Company (Bevel)
Photo: Wikimedia Commons · Wikimedia

Picture a kid from the Queensbridge housing projects who reads Bonfire of the Vanities, decides he's going to get rich, and somehow ends up not on Wall Street but in a Palo Alto lab obsessing over the physics of a single-blade razor. That's Tristan Walker, and his origin story is the kind of plot twist that would feel too neat if it weren't true.

From Wall Street Dreams to the Grooming Aisle

Walker hustled early, working on a trading floor before he could legally drink. But the corner-office life didn't stick. He talked his way into Foursquare as its head of business development during the check-in app's hype years, then did a stint as an entrepreneur-in-residence at the legendary venture firm Andreessen Horowitz. That's where the real lightbulb flickered on.

The insight was almost embarrassingly obvious once he said it out loud: razors built for straight, fine hair wreck coarse, curly hair. Razor bumps. Ingrown hairs. An entire demographic of Black men quietly suffering through a daily ritual that big brands had never bothered to design for. So Walker built Bevel, a single-blade razor system engineered specifically for the people the grooming aisle ignored.

Design as a Love Letter

Here's where Walker's genius really shows. Bevel didn't look like a drugstore product. The packaging was matte, premium, deliberate, the kind of thing you'd leave out on the sink as decor. The whole brand world felt like it belonged in a design museum, not buried on a bottom shelf next to dusty aftershave.

Walker treated an underserved market not as a niche to tolerate, but as an audience to celebrate.

He framed Walker & Company around a simple mission: making health and beauty simple for people of color. That clarity was magnetic. The marketing felt like culture, not advertising, and customers became evangelists. Bevel expanded from razors into a full lineup of trimmers, skincare, and the wildly popular FORM hair care line, each one carrying that same considered, grown-up aesthetic.

The Exit That Made History

The growth got loud enough that the biggest player in the room came knocking. In 2018, consumer-goods titan Procter & Gamble acquired Walker & Company, a deal reportedly worth a sum in the tens of millions, with terms kept private. It was widely reported as one of the first times a major CPG conglomerate scooped up a Black-founded, mission-driven brand of its kind, and Walker stayed on as CEO of an independent subsidiary.

What makes the story sing isn't just the payday. It's that Walker proved a market everyone else treated as an afterthought was actually a goldmine of loyalty and design opportunity. He didn't chase a trend; he served real people with real needs and made the product gorgeous while doing it.

He's since launched other ventures, relocated his operation to Atlanta to build outside the Valley bubble, and become a sharp, frequently-quoted voice on representation in venture capital and who gets funded in the first place. But Bevel remains the blueprint: see the people nobody's looking at, make something beautiful for them, price it like it matters, and let the loyalty compound into something the giants can't ignore.

The deeper lesson of Walker's run is about respect as a business strategy. He never positioned Bevel as charity or as a compromise; he positioned it as better, full stop, for the customer it was built around. That confidence is exactly what turned a single-blade razor into a category-defining brand. Build for the overlooked, and you'll never run out of customers.
