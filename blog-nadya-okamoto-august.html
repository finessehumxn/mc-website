Nadya Okamoto Made Periods Loud on Purpose

She fought period poverty as a teen, then built a Gen-Z femcare brand that refuses to whisper.

Nadya OkamotoAugust
Photo: Larry D. Moore · CC BY 4.0 · Wikimedia

For roughly forever, the period products industry sold its goods with blue liquid, hushed tones, and packaging designed to be smuggled into a bathroom undetected. Nadya Okamoto looked at that entire playbook and said: absolutely not. Then she built a brand that turns the volume all the way up. Periods, but make them a personality.

The Teenage Activist Era

Nadya's come-up started in adversity. As a teenager, her family experienced a stretch of housing instability, and conversations with women at nearby shelters opened her eyes to period poverty, the brutal, under-discussed reality that countless people can't afford the products they need every month. At sixteen, she did what most sixteen-year-olds don't: she co-founded a nonprofit to distribute period products and fight the stigma head-on. It grew into one of the larger youth-driven movements in the space, and Nadya became a loud, unembarrassed voice for menstrual equity while she was still, technically, a kid.

The radical idea was simple: stop treating a normal bodily function like a dirty secret.

Building August

Activism taught Nadya the problem. Building August let her attack it commercially. Launched to reimagine the period experience for a new generation, August isn't designed to disappear into a purse. The branding is bold, warm, and unapologetically out loud, with a community-first ethos and packaging that looks like something you'd want on the shelf, not hidden under the sink. The whole identity rejects the apologetic, sanitized aesthetic the category was drowning in.

The creativity is in the reframe. August treats its customers as a community to build with, not a demographic to market at. Nadya leaned hard into content, building enormous social media followings and turning candid, sometimes gleefully oversharing conversations about menstruation into the brand's engine. She made talking about periods feel less like a PSA and more like a group chat. That's marketing genius dressed up as honesty, and it's exactly what the old guard could never pull off.

Scaling the Stigma-Smasher

The mission stayed stitched into the model. August built in giving and advocacy components so growth and impact moved together rather than competing. The brand attracted real investment, expanded distribution, and positioned itself as a leader in the new wave of Gen-Z-native femcare companies challenging the legacy giants who'd had the aisle to themselves for generations.

What makes Nadya a genuine case study isn't just that she sells a good product. It's that she identified a cultural problem, the shame, attacked it as both an activist and a brand-builder, and made the two reinforce each other. Every loud, transparent piece of content chips at the stigma and grows the company. Few founders manage to make their values and their growth strategy the same exact lever.

So consider the Okamoto blueprint. Find the thing everyone's too embarrassed to talk about, then talk about it louder than anyone ever has. Build the brand the world was too polite to make, and let the honesty do the selling.
