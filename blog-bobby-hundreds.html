The Law-School Dropout Who Sold a Lifestyle in a T-Shirt

Bobby Hundreds blogged, sketched, and storytold his way into streetwear royalty, one Adam Bomb at a time.

Bobby Kim (Bobby Hundreds)The Hundreds

Bobby Kim was supposed to be a lawyer. He had the law-school plan, the immigrant-kid expectations, the whole respectable script. Instead he and his partner Ben started a brand out of a blog and a punk-rock attitude, and somewhere along the way The Hundreds became a cornerstone of modern streetwear. The dropout move worked out.

The Blog Was the Real Product

Back in 2003, when streetwear meant exclusive drops and zero explanation, Bobby did the opposite. He wrote. Constantly. He blogged about the scene, the shops, the people, the philosophy behind the brand, before "content" was a marketing word. The clothes were great, but the storytelling was the moat.

That's the part aspiring founders sleep on. The Hundreds didn't just sell graphic tees, it sold a worldview, a community, a running narrative you wanted to be part of. The mascot, the Adam Bomb, that grinning cartoon bomb, wasn't just a logo. It was a flag for a tribe. People didn't buy a hat. They bought membership.

Slow Hype, Built by Hand

While other brands chased mass distribution, Bobby leaned into scarcity and authenticity. The Hundreds opened flagship shops in LA, San Francisco, New York, turning retail into clubhouses. Collaborations with everyone from snack brands to cartoons kept the world expanding without diluting it. Every drop felt like a chapter, not a transaction.

Hype fades. A story people want to live inside is what actually compounds.

His design instinct was always about meaning over flash. An Adam Bomb tee carried a wink, a reference, a piece of the brand's ongoing mythology. He understood that in streetwear the graphic is the message, and the message is the brand. That tight loop between art, writing, and product is why the label outlived a hundred flash-in-the-pan competitors.

From Garage Brand to Culture Authority

Over two decades, The Hundreds grew from a scrappy LA operation into a globally respected name, reportedly building a multimillion-dollar business along the way. Bobby further cemented his elder-statesman status with his memoir This Is Not a T-Shirt, which doubles as a love letter to building brands the honest, slow, community-first way.

What makes him a Millennial Creatives hero is that he treated branding as literature. He wrote the brand into existence, post by post, drop by drop, until the audience felt like co-authors. He proved you don't need a viral fluke or a venture check to build something lasting, you need a point of view and the stamina to keep showing up.

In a world obsessed with going viral, Bobby Hundreds bet on going deep, and that's the blueprint that still holds.
