The Brain Behind the Kardashian Fashion Machine

Emma Grede co-built two billion-dollar brands and made denim that actually fits. Respect.

Emma GredeGood American & SKIMS
Photo: Janeirohy · CC BY 4.0 · Wikimedia

You know the brands. You might not know the woman who built them. Emma Grede is the co-founder and CEO of Good American, the co-founder of SKIMS alongside Kim Kardashian, and the first Black woman to ever guest-invest on the US version of Dragons' Den, known stateside as Shark Tank. She is, quietly, one of the most powerful operators in fashion. Loudly, actually. She's not that quiet about it, and good for her.

From East London to the A-List

Grede grew up in East London, raised by a single mother, and left school at sixteen. No fashion-dynasty inheritance, no Ivy League runway to a corner office. She clawed her way up through the entertainment-marketing world, building businesses that connected brands with talent, learning the machinery of fame from the inside before she ever launched a product of her own.

That backstage education turned out to be the whole point. She understood something most designers don't: a brand isn't just clothes, it's a story, a relationship, and a deeply unsexy supply chain that has to actually deliver.

Building the Brands That Fit Everybody

In 2016, Grede co-founded Good American with Khloe Kardashian. The pitch was refreshingly simple and a little bit furious: denim that actually fits real women, across a full range of sizes, with no apology and no separate sad corner of the store. Good American launched with the most inclusive size range in the category and reportedly pulled in seven figures on its very first day. The product solved a real problem, and the market noticed immediately.

Then came SKIMS, the shapewear and loungewear juggernaut she co-founded with Kim Kardashian in 2019. SKIMS took the same philosophy, every shade, every size, gorgeous minimalist branding, and turned it into a cultural phenomenon. The product drops sold out in minutes. The brand reportedly reached a valuation in the billions, becoming one of the buzziest companies in fashion.

Grede's genius is treating inclusivity as a business strategy, not a slogan. Serve everyone properly, and everyone shows up to buy.

The Operator's Edge

Here's what makes Grede different from a celebrity face on a label: she runs the company. The branding, the launch strategy, the relentless drops that create scarcity and hype, the careful design of products that photograph beautifully and fit honestly, that's her craft. She married Kardashian-level fame with genuine operational muscle, and the combination is nearly unbeatable.

She's also become a mentor and outspoken advocate, pushing for more diversity in boardrooms and more women, especially women of color, getting funded and getting seats at the table. She knows exactly how rare her path is, and she's loud about widening it.

The scale speaks for itself: two brands tied to billion-dollar momentum, a TV platform, and a reputation as the operator other founders dream of working with. Not bad for a sixteen-year-old who left school with a plan she hadn't even written yet.

The lesson: fame opens the door, but it's the design, the fit, and the unglamorous machinery behind the curtain that builds an empire that lasts.
