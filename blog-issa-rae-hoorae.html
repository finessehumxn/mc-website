Issa Rae Built an Empire Out of Awkwardness

A scrappy web series about being awkward and Black became a multi-armed media machine.

Issa RaeHOORAE & Raedio
Photo: Bea Phi · CC BY-SA 4.0 · Wikimedia

In 2011, a young creator named Issa Rae uploaded a low-budget web series called The Misadventures of Awkward Black Girl to YouTube. No studio, no budget, no permission. Just a sharp voice telling a story Hollywood had spent decades insisting nobody wanted to watch. Fifteen years later she's a mogul with a media company, a record label, and a habit of betting on herself and winning. Awkward, it turns out, scales beautifully.

The DIY Years

Awkward Black Girl wasn't supposed to be a launchpad. It was supposed to be the show Issa wished existed, made with whatever she had on hand. But it hit a nerve, racking up a devoted audience and proving the thing executives kept doubting: there was a massive, hungry crowd for stories about ordinary Black life that weren't trauma, weren't tokenism, just funny and human and specific. The series was crowd-funded by fans who got it before the industry did.

She didn't wait for a seat at the table. She built her own table on the internet and invited everyone over.

HBO and the Awkward Renaissance

That internet groundswell turned into Insecure, the HBO series Issa co-created and starred in, which ran for five acclaimed seasons and became a cultural touchstone, fashion, music, the brunch discourse, all of it. Crucially, Issa used the show as a platform, putting on emerging directors, writers, and especially musicians, treating the soundtrack like a star in its own right. Insecure wasn't just a hit. It was a proof of concept for an entire creative philosophy: open the door behind you.

The creativity was never only on screen. It was in the architecture. Issa saw that one show could be the seed of an ecosystem if you owned the infrastructure instead of renting it.

The Empire: HOORAE and Raedio

So she built the infrastructure. HOORAE is her media company, an umbrella spanning film, television, and digital that develops projects across formats and keeps the pipeline of new talent flowing. Underneath it sits Raedio, her audio everywhere company and record label that turns music supervision into a business, breaking artists through placements and building a catalog instead of just licensing other people's hits. She signed a major deal with WarnerMedia, invested in ventures including a stake in a beverage brand and a hair-care line, and even opened a coffee shop in her old LA neighborhood. Reports peg her net worth comfortably in the multi-millions, and the empire keeps branching.

What ties it together is the same instinct from the YouTube days. Issa identifies talent and stories the mainstream overlooks, then builds the platform to deliver them at scale, and owns a piece of the upside while she's at it. The kid making a web series in her apartment and the executive greenlighting slates are running the exact same playbook, just with more zeros.

The lesson is gloriously simple. You don't need the industry's permission to start, and once you've got momentum, you build the doors instead of knocking on them. Make the thing you wish existed, then own the house it lives in.
